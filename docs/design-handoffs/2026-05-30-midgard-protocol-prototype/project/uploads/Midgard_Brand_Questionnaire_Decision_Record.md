# Midgard Brand Questionnaire Decision Record

Prepared: 2026-05-28

Status: Reconstructed decision record from the brand questionnaire work, saved brand direction, and follow-up implementation corrections. This is not a verbatim transcript; it is a team-readable record of the questions asked, Dave's captured answers, and the decisions that resulted.

## Executive Summary

Midgard's next-phase brand direction centers on a Category Rebel voice with Confident Challenger sharpness. The primary audience is builders and partners, with Users, Builders, and Partners as plain audience paths. The emotional promise is trust: explain why the system should be trusted before selling speed, scale, or momentum.

The visual world is Dark Ancient Tech / Mythic Tree World. Trust Architecture is the mechanism layer inside that world. The correct visual execution is not a crude stick-frame tree; it is an ancient technical world-tree system made of stone, gold circuitry, branching trust paths, settlement bedrock, proof apertures, and controlled Midgard green verification signal.

The first production package is brand guidelines plus a 12 to 15 slide launch deck spec, then selected key slides can become higher-end Figma/design specs and coded prototypes.

## How To Read This

Each item below includes:

- Question: the consultant-style question asked during the brand direction process.
- Dave's answer: the captured answer or decision.
- Resulting decision: how that answer shaped the brand package.

## 1. Strategy And Category

### 1. What role should Midgard play in the category?

Dave's answer: Midgard should challenge ETH L2 fragmentation and generic rollup thinking, but with mechanism and evidence behind the challenge.

Resulting decision: Use a Category Rebel voice, but avoid cheap shots. Diagnose structural category problems rather than attacking specific competitors or users.

### 2. How sharp should the brand be?

Dave's answer: Sharp enough to feel like a challenger, but not reckless or unserious.

Resulting decision: Use Confident Challenger sharpness. The tone mix is 70 direct / 30 defiant.

### 3. Who matters most for the next phase?

Dave's answer: Builders and partners are the primary audience.

Resulting decision: The launch deck, gateway, homepage, and partner materials should be builder/partner-first, while still supporting Users, Builders, and Partners as separate paths.

### 4. What should the audience feel?

Dave's answer: Cardano-native scaling should feel like it is becoming inevitable because the architecture earns trust.

Resulting decision: The brand should create inevitability through mechanism, proof, and clarity, not hype or unsupported claims.

### 5. What should Midgard lead with: speed, trust, migration, or something else?

Dave's answer: Trust comes first. Explain why the system should be trusted before selling speed or hype.

Resulting decision: The core story posture is Trust Architecture. The deck should use a trust-first story arc, but that arc is not the deck name.

## 2. Voice And Tone

### 6. What is the core voice?

Dave's answer: Category Rebel.

Resulting decision: Midgard can name a category problem and take a point of view, but every challenge must be grounded in mechanism.

### 7. What is the sharpness level?

Dave's answer: Confident Challenger.

Resulting decision: The brand can be provocative, especially in category diagnosis, founder voice, and major campaign moments.

### 8. What is the tone mix?

Dave's answer: 70 direct / 30 defiant.

Resulting decision: Most copy should be clean, exact, and useful. The defiant edge appears selectively.

### 9. What should the copy shape be?

Dave's answer: Sharp thesis.

Resulting decision: Headlines and slide openers should make one clear point. No long warm-up before the thesis.

### 10. What should the signature style be?

Dave's answer: Plain spoken.

Resulting decision: Even when visuals are mythic, the copy stays direct and practical.

### 11. How should jargon be handled?

Dave's answer: Translate first, then name.

Resulting decision: Explain the mechanism in normal language before using technical labels.

### 12. What voice risk should we avoid most?

Dave's answer: No cheap shots.

Resulting decision: Do not mock ecosystems, competitors, or user choices. Diagnose structure, not people.

### 13. Can founder voice be sharper?

Dave's answer: Yes, when clearly founder-led or signed.

Resulting decision: Founder voice can carry more edge. The main brand voice stays measured and proof-led.

### 14. What should social tone feel like?

Dave's answer: Technical Provocateur.

Resulting decision: Social can be sharper, but should still be useful, source-aware, and grounded in mechanism.

## 3. Language Territories

### 15. What is the tagline territory?

Dave's answer: Trust The Path.

Resulting decision: Use Trust The Path as a brand territory and deck/gateway theme, not as a slogan repeated everywhere.

### 16. What is the CTA style?

Dave's answer: Verify Then Build.

Resulting decision: Use Verify Then Build as the primary builder/partner CTA style.

### 17. What is the public-safe migration campaign frame?

Dave's answer: Secure Migration.

Resulting decision: Use Secure Migration externally. Do not use internal vampire-attack language in public materials.

### 18. What should the deck arc be?

Dave's answer: trust before build momentum. This was captured as a language/theme direction, not a name.

Resulting decision: The launch deck sequence should establish trust, show mechanism, then ask builders/partners to engage.

### 19. What should normal readers hear first?

Dave's answer: Why Trust It.

Resulting decision: The user path should answer why to trust Midgard before asking users to take action.

## 4. Audience Paths

### 20. Should Midgard have one voice or separate voices by persona?

Dave's answer: One coherent Midgard voice with persona modes.

Resulting decision: Keep one brand voice, but adapt depth and emphasis by audience.

### 21. What are the audience paths?

Dave's answer: Users, Builders, Partners.

Resulting decision: Use plain labels. Do not invent lore names for audience paths.

### 22. What does the Users path need to answer?

Dave's answer: Why trust it?

Resulting decision: Users get clear explanation, safety/status context, support routes, and official channels.

### 23. What does the Builders path need to answer?

Dave's answer: Can I verify then build?

Resulting decision: Builders get architecture, constraints, docs, examples, Midgard Builder Readiness, technical working sessions, and office hours.

### 24. What does the Partners path need to answer?

Dave's answer: What role can we play safely?

Resulting decision: Partners enter through functional readiness tracks first. Co-marketing comes later and only after approval.

## 5. Security, Trust, And Claims

### 25. How bold should security language be?

Dave's answer: Bold But Qualified.

Resulting decision: Strongly emphasize architecture, settlement, proof paths, challenge mechanics, and Cardano L1 anchoring, while naming current limits.

### 26. How should Cardano L1 anchoring be handled?

Dave's answer: It should be central, but claim strength must stay approved.

Resulting decision: Use Cardano L1 as bedrock, rail, or settlement layer visually. Public copy inherits claim strength from the claims matrix.

### 27. How should testnet and mainnet status be handled?

Dave's answer: Separate what is live, planned, simulated, testnet, and mainnet.

Resulting decision: Use confidence labels, demonstration-data labels, and source/status chips.

### 28. How should token/governance-token language be handled?

Dave's answer: Do not mention tokens publicly at this stage; internally, begin planning a retroactive-impact token model that learns from Optimism.

Resulting decision: Public token, reward, airdrop, points, yield, eligibility, and governance claims must stay out of launch materials unless token policy, counsel, and leadership approve exact language.

### 29. How should ETH L2 comparisons be handled?

Dave's answer: Challenge fragmentation and generic rollup thinking, but do not overreach.

Resulting decision: Category diagnosis is allowed. Unsupported superiority claims and named attacks are not.

## 6. Visual Direction

### 30. What is the visual world?

Dave's answer: Mythic Tree World with Ancient Tech styling.

Resulting decision: Use Dark Ancient Tech / Mythic Tree World as the visual system.

### 31. What is the palette?

Dave's answer: Mostly dark with Midgard green, muted gold, stone, and deep ink.

Resulting decision: Gold should be visibly tied to proof, settlement, activation, and trust-path circuitry. Green should be the controlled trust signal.

### 32. What should the visual feeling be?

Dave's answer: Mythic but not fantasy-game.

Resulting decision: The visual system can be cinematic and mythic, but the interface and copy stay technical, credible, and useful.

### 33. What should be avoided visually?

Dave's answer: No generic crypto, no purple-blue gradient futurism, no fantasy-game motifs, no stock tech, no obvious AI artifacts, no cheap overdecorated diagrams.

Resulting decision: Use beautiful schematics, stone, gold circuitry, proof labels, rails, topology, and controlled motion.

### 34. What should the Cinematic Gateway do?

Dave's answer: Deliver the high-emotion world-tree moment before the normal homepage.

Resulting decision: The gateway gets minimal copy and a cinematic mythic world-tree trust-path visual.

### 35. What should the homepage or second page do?

Dave's answer: Show advanced living system visuals: tree/topology, transaction routes, batching, settlement, proofs, and simulated data first.

Resulting decision: The second page is a Living Trust Architecture system with inspectable layers.

### 36. What should child pages and docs do?

Dave's answer: Use quiet motion and clear facts.

Resulting decision: Docs can be full-brand, but prose and code remain primary.

### 37. What are the texture routes?

Dave's answer: Preserve Stone + Light and Organic Rootwork for later comparison.

Resulting decision: Stone + Light supports permanence and executive seriousness. Organic Rootwork supports system-alive energy and the Mythic Tree World premise.

### 38. How should the older colorful Tree World handoff be treated?

Dave's answer: Preserve it as an option, but this new direction is separate.

Resulting decision: The older colorful handoff remains reference-only. It does not overwrite the darker trust-architecture branch.

### 39. What correction did Dave make about the tree theme?

Dave's answer: The issue was the crude stick-frame tree execution, not the tree theme itself. The ancient tech world is still tree-themed.

Resulting decision: The correct rule is: keep Mythic Tree World, but execute it as Dark Ancient Tech and trust architecture. Ban stick-frame trees, literal forests, wooden trunks, leafy branches, and fantasy nature.

## 7. Motion And Interaction

### 40. How much motion should the brand use?

Dave's answer: Motion should impress, but must remain performant and optional.

Resulting decision: Build static fallback first. Respect reduced-motion settings.

### 41. Should the site use forced guided scroll?

Dave's answer: No. Prefer Natural Reveal.

Resulting decision: Content should reveal as users move normally through the page.

### 42. Should users be able to inspect the system?

Dave's answer: Yes. Explore mode should inspect key layers.

Resulting decision: Prioritize Trust Layers as the main system view: activity, batching, proof, challenge, settlement, Cardano L1.

### 43. What should mobile feel like?

Dave's answer: Intentional and premium, not a cropped desktop.

Resulting decision: Every hero, diagram, and interaction needs a mobile-safe treatment.

### 44. Should rich media use audio?

Dave's answer: Video only, no default audio.

Resulting decision: Motion tests should be visual only by default.

## 8. Launch Deck And First Package

### 45. What should the first concrete deliverables be?

Dave's answer: Brand guidelines plus a 12 to 15 slide launch deck spec.

Resulting decision: First production package is brand guidelines plus launch deck spec, not final public copy.

### 46. What format should the deck use first?

Dave's answer: Editable PPTX first, then Figma/design specs for key slides.

Resulting decision: Build reviewable PPTX first. Upgrade selected key slides later.

### 47. How long should the launch deck be?

Dave's answer: 12 to 15 slides.

Resulting decision: The deck spec supports a 14-slide core version and optional appendix.

### 48. What is the deck proof/emotion ratio?

Dave's answer: 70 proof / 30 myth.

Resulting decision: Cinematic visuals open and close the story, while the body uses diagrams, proof objects, readiness matrices, and status labels.

### 49. What credibility order should the deck use?

Dave's answer: Architecture first.

Resulting decision: Explain the mechanism before partner asks, campaign asks, or hype.

### 50. Should v1 include final sample copy?

Dave's answer: No sample copy in v1.

Resulting decision: v1 should include structure, intent, voice rules, slide purpose, and proof objects.

## 9. AI-Assisted Production Workflow

### 51. Who owns first production?

Dave's answer: Internal AI-led first, using best tools where helpful but minimizing tool sprawl.

Resulting decision: Codex/Claude orchestrate specs, prompts, deck outlines, code prototypes, and production routing.

### 52. Which tools belong in the production stack?

Dave's answer: Codex/Claude, Midjourney or equivalent, Runway Gen-4 or Luma Ray/Dream Machine, Spline or Three.js, Rive, Figma/Figma Make/Relume, PPTX/Figma.

Resulting decision: Each tool gets a specific role in the production handoff.

### 53. Can AI-generated public assets be used?

Dave's answer: Yes, only if commercial-safe enough, high-quality, original-looking, and free of obvious AI artifacts.

Resulting decision: Do not spotlight AI usage publicly. Keep prompts, sources, candidates, licenses, and edit decisions in a production log.

Dave's answer: No. All new designs from this direction except the base logo.

Resulting decision: The only inherited visual asset allowed by default is the base Midgard logo.

### 55. What exactly should be custom-built?

Dave's answer: Cinematic pages, diagrams, interactions, deck layouts, proof objects, motion treatments, and web prototype.

Resulting decision: Final production should not depend on old DRep/AlphaGrowth visuals, old deck skill artifacts, or older generated structures.

### 56. How should the team port this work?

Dave's answer: Create a full portable package with marketing, voice, brand, tone, design details, tool handoffs, assets, deck, and prototype.

Resulting decision: Use `Midgard_Portable_Brand_Package_2026-05-28/` as the portable team handoff package.

## 12. Final Direction In One Paragraph

Midgard should present itself as a Category Rebel and Confident Challenger for Cardano-native scaling: plain-spoken, technical, and trust-first, with enough defiant edge to challenge fragmented rollup thinking without becoming a cheap-shot brand. The visual world is Dark Ancient Tech / Mythic Tree World, where trust architecture appears as branching paths, proof circuitry, settlement rails, and Cardano L1 bedrock. Builders and partners are the primary audience, Users/Builders/Partners remain the plain audience paths, and every public claim must stay bold but qualified until approved.
